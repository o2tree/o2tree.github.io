All organisms require nutrients to live and grow, and those living in the open ocean are no exception. Tiny phytoplankton drift on the water’s surface, converting sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide from the atmosphere into food and oxygen. Like other photosynthetic organisms, such as land plants, these tiny algae and cyanobacteria require nutrients for photosynthesis to occur. **On land, nitrogen and phosphorous are often the limiting nutrients, but in many parts of the open ocean, these nutrients are generally available.** It’s other, trace nutrients that are missing, and iron is one of the key players in this system. Adding small amounts of iron to the ocean’s surface can trigger a bloom of phytoplankton big enough to be seen from space. Such additions happen naturally, such as when winds blow dust from the Sahara Desert or ash from a volcanic eruption. When the plume of dust or ash settles over the ocean’s surface, it triggers massive blooms of phytoplankton that remove substantial amounts of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. Iron fertilization is a Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR) technique that would mimic this natural system, artificially adding iron to the ocean’s surface to stimulate growth of phytoplankton Limes soils are often iron deficit, even when sufficient amounts of iron are present. This is because of the generally high pH value, which leads to iron precipitation. # The Conway medium is a classic and widely used fertilizer formulation for growing marine microalgae in aquaculture hatcheries. It provides a balanced mix of major nutrients (Nitrogen, Phosphorus) and essential trace metals and vitamins. Unlike a single product, "Conway fertilizer" refers to a recipe. You can either purchase it as a pre-made kit or mix it yourself from laboratory-grade chemicals. --- ### What is Conway Medium? It is a defined, chemical solution used to enrich seawater to create the perfect "soup" for growing specific species of microalgae (e.g., *Isochrysis*, *Chaetoceros*, *Tetraselmis*). It was developed by the renowned algal physiologist W. A. P. Conway. ### Composition of Conway Medium The classic recipe is divided into three separate stock solutions to prevent chemicals from reacting with each other and precipitating during storage. --- #### Solution A (Macronutrients) * Function: Provides the main building blocks for algal growth (Nitrogen, Phosphorus). * Typical Composition (per 1 liter of fresh water): * Ammonium Nitrate (NH₄NO₃) or Sodium Nitrate (NaNO₃): 50 - 100 g * Sodium Dihydrogen Phosphate (NaH₂PO₄ · 2H₂O): 5 - 10 g * Dosage: 1 mL of Solution A is added per 1 liter of algae culture. ---- #### Solution B (Trace Metals + Iron) * Function: Provides essential micronutrients, including chelated iron. * Typical Composition (per 1 liter of fresh water): * Iron Chelate (Fe-EDTA or Fe-Citrate): 5 - 10 g (This is your bioavailable iron source) * Manganese Chloride (MnCl₂ · 4H₂O): 0.5 g * Boric Acid (H₃BO₃): 5 g * Zinc Sulfate (ZnSO₄ · 7H₂O): 0.5 g * Cobalt Nitrate (Co(NO₃)₂ · 6H₂O): 0.1 g * Copper Sulfate (CuSO₄ · 5H₂O): 0.02 g * Sodium Molybdate (Na₂MoO₄ · 2H₂O): 0.1 g * Dosage: 1 mL of Solution B is added per 1 liter of algae culture. --- #### Solution C (Vitamins) * Function: Provides essential vitamins that many algae cannot synthesize themselves. * Typical Composition (per 1 liter of fresh water): * Vitamin B12 (Cyanocobalamin): 0.1 g * Vitamin B1 (Thiamine HCl): 1.0 g * Biotin: 0.01 g * Important: This solution is highly perishable. It must be stored in a refrigerator and made fresh every 1-2 months. * Dosage: 1 mL of Solution C is added per 1 liter of algae culture. --- ### How to Use Conway Medium 1. Prepare Stock Solutions: Dissolve the chemicals for Solutions A, B, and C in separate 1-liter bottles of distilled or RO water. 2. Sterilize (Optional but Recommended): Filter each solution through a 0.2-micron filter to prevent bacterial contamination. Solution C should not be autoclaved (heat will destroy the vitamins). 3. Enrich the Seawater: * Fill your algae culture tank with filtered and sterilized seawater. * For every 1 liter of seawater, add: * 1 mL of Solution A * 1 mL of Solution B * 1 mL of Solution C * Example: For a 100-liter culture tank, add 100 mL of each solution. 4. Inoculate: Add your pure algae culture (the "starter") to the fertilized seawater. 5. Aerate and Illuminate: Provide constant light and aeration to promote growth. --- ### Where to Get Conway Fertilizer Products You have two options: #### 1. Pre-Mixed Kits (Recommended for Beginners/Small Operations) This is the easiest and most reliable method. You can buy convenient kits online from aquaculture supply companies. They typically provide pre-measured packets of powders to make each stock solution. Recommended Indonesian & International Suppliers: * PT. Search for "Conway Medium kit" or "Algae Fertilizer kit". #### 2. Mix Your Own (For Larger Operations This is an advanced but crucial topic for optimizing microalgae growth. Calculating the final pH of a fertilizer mixture is complex because it involves acid-base chemistry, ion interactions, and the buffering capacity of seawater. However, we can establish a practical framework for managing pH in your hatchery. ### The Core Challenge You are mixing two things: 1. Seawater: A highly buffered solution at ~pH 8.1-8.2. Its carbonate-bicarbonate system (CO₃²⁻/HCO₃⁻) resists pH changes. 2. Fertilizer Solutions: These are often acidic (especially the trace metal solution B). The final pH is not a simple average. It depends on the buffering capacity of your specific seawater and the quantity and acidity of the fertilizer added. --- ### Step-by-Step Practical Guide (Instead of Pure Calculation) For a hatchery, a practical, iterative approach is more effective than a theoretical calculation. #### 1. The Goal pH Range Most marine microalgae (e.g., *Chaetoceros*, *Tetraselmis*, *Isochrysis*) thrive in a pH range of 7.8 - 8.5. #### 2. The Practical Method: Measure and Adjust This is the most reliable method for a hatchery setting. What you need: * A calibrated pH meter (essential tool). * A fresh batch of your filtered seawater. * Your stock fertilizer solutions (Conway A, B, C). Procedure: 1. Measure Initial pH: Take a 1-liter sample of your filtered seawater. Measure and record its pH. (e.g., pH 8.2) 2. Add Full Fertilizer Dose: Add the standard dose of your fertilizers to the 1-liter sample (e.g., 1 mL of Solution A, 1 mL of Solution B, 1 mL of Solution C). Stir well. 3. Measure Final pH: Wait a few minutes for the reading to stabilize, then measure the pH again. (e.g., It might drop to pH 7.9). 4. Interpret the Result: * If pH is within 7.8-8.5 (e.g., 7.9): Perfect. Proceed with inoculating your algae. * If pH is too low (< 7.8): Your fertilizer mix is too acidic for your local seawater's buffering capacity. * If pH is too high (> 8.5): Unlikely when adding fertilizer, but possible if your source water is unusual. #### 3. How to Adjust the Final pH If pH is too LOW after fertilization: * Solution: Dilute the Fertilizer Dose. * This is the safest and most common method. * Example: If adding 1 mL/L of each solution caused a larger-than-desired drop, try adding only 0.7 mL/L of Solution B (the most acidic one, containing trace metals). Keep Solutions A and C at 1 mL/L. * Re-test the pH with this new ratio. Find the dose that gives you the target pH. Algae growth might be slightly slower but more stable than in a low-pH, stressful environment. If adjustment is absolutely necessary (use cautiously): * To Raise pH (if too low): Use a very dilute solution of Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) or Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO₃). Add dropwise to the fertilized seawater while stirring and monitoring the pH meter. Bicarbonate is safer and adds carbon, which algae can use. * To Lower pH (if too high): Use a very dilute solution of Hydrochloric Acid (HCl). This is rarely needed after fertilizing. Warning: Avoid using these strong acids/bases directly in your main culture tank. Always adjust a small sample first to determine the required amount. --- ### Understanding the "Why": The Chemistry of Conway Solutions * Solution A (Macronutrients): Generally near neutral pH. Has minimal impact. * Solution B (Trace Metals): This is the most acidic solution. The metal salts (especially Iron-EDTA) hydrolyze in water, releasing H⁺ ions and lowering the pH significantly. * Solution C (Vitamins): Minimal impact on pH. ### Example Workflow for a New Seawater Source Let's say you have a new batch of seawater and a standard Conway recipe. 1. Seawater pH = 8.2 2. Add 1 mL/L each of A, B, C. 3. Final pH = 7.6 *(Too low!)* 4. Action: Create a new test liter. Add 1 mL/L of A, 0.5 mL/L of B, and 1 mL/L of C. 5. New Final pH = 7.9 *(Acceptable!)* 6. Conclusion: For this specific seawater batch, you will use a reduced dose of Solution B (0.5 mL per liter) to maintain optimal pH. ## [High pressure (30.40 inHg and higher) and Low Pressure (29.60 inHg or lower) on Ocean about fish](https://wildhunter.ie/blogs/fishing-blog/effects-of-air-pressure-on-fish) Barometric pressure is the weight of the atmosphere in a specific area, usually measured in millibars (mb) and inches of Mercury (inHg). A number of factors can influence the barometric pressure in a given area, including altitude, wind patterns, and temperature. * **A normal barometric pressure reading is typically between 29.70 inHg and 30.40 inHg** * when there’s low barometric pressure and less pressure on a fish’s body, the bladder expands. Fish may swim deeper where the water pressure is higher as a way to adjust, becoming less interested in feeding until conditions normalize. * As we mentioned above, most agree that fishing is slow on both high-pressure and low-pressure days.** If the barometric pressure is above 30.50 inHg or below 29.60 inHg**, you might be better off waiting until the pressure normalizes. * High Pressure (Good Weather): During periods of high atmospheric pressure, the air pressing down on the water is greater, slightly compressing the water. Fish with swim bladders experience a slight reduction in the organ’s volume, making them naturally sink a bit. To counteract this, they expend energy adjusting their position in the water column, which can make them less active or cautious, influencing their feeding and movement. * Low Pressure (Bad Weather): When low-pressure systems approach, such as before a storm, the pressure exerted on the water decreases. Magnesium hydroxide is another compound used in water chemistry, and it's an excellent follow-up question in the context of raising pH. Here’s a detailed breakdown of magnesium hydroxide in aquarium use and how it compares to calcium hydroxide. What is Magnesium Hydroxide? · Chemical Formula: Mg(OH)₂ · Common Forms: · Pharmaceutical Grade: Sold as a liquid antacid under names like "Milk of Magnesia." This form is pure and safe for aquarium use if it contains no additives (e.g., flavorings, sodium). · Powdered Form: A fine white powder, often used in water treatment. · How it Works: Like calcium hydroxide, it is a strong base. When added to water, it dissociates and releases hydroxide ions (OH⁻), which directly neutralize acid (H⁺ ions), thereby raising the pH. --- Comparison: Magnesium Hydroxide vs. Calcium Hydroxide (Kalkwasser) While both raise pH, they have critical differences that affect their use in aquariums. Feature Magnesium Hydroxide (Mg(OH)₂) Calcium Hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂ - Kalkwasser) Solubility Extremely Low (~0.00064 g/100mL) Very Low (~0.173 g/100mL) Relative Strength Weaker base. Raises pH more slowly and gradually. Stronger base. Raises pH more quickly and potently. Primary Aquarium Use 1. Raising pH gently. 2. Supplementing Magnesium. 1. Raising and maintaining pH. 2. Supplementing Calcium and Alkalinity. What it Adds Magnesium (Mg²⁺) and Hydroxide (OH⁻) Calcium (Ca²⁺) and Hydroxide (OH⁻) Reaction Speed Very Slow. Due to its extremely low solubility, it acts as a sustained, slow-release source of hydroxide. Fast (relative to Mg(OH)₂). Its higher solubility means it affects water chemistry more immediately. Key Implications for Your Aquarium: 1. Slower, Gentler pH Rise: Because of its extremely low solubility, magnesium hydroxide is less likely to cause a dangerous rapid pH spike compared to calcium hydroxide. This can make it seem "safer," but it also makes it much less effective for actively raising pH in a meaningful time frame. 2. It Adds Magnesium, Not Calcium: This is its most important function in reef aquaria. In a marine system, high calcium and alkalinity levels can cause calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) to precipitate out of the water uncontrollably. Magnesium inhibits this precipitation. Therefore, magnesium is a crucial element that must be maintained (~1250-1350 ppm) to keep calcium and alkalinity stable. 3. Not a Primary Buffer: Magnesium hydroxide is not an efficient way to boost carbonate hardness (KH). While it raises pH, it doesn't directly add carbonates or bicarbonates (the KH buffer). Your KH level would remain unchanged, meaning the pH rise could be unstable without adequate buffering capacity. --- How to Use Magnesium Hydroxide Safely (If You Choose To) If you want to use it for a gentle pH nudge, you must use a pure, additive-free product. Procedure: 1. Source: Use only pure, unscented, unflavored Milk of Magnesia (check the ingredients list: it should only contain water and magnesium hydroxide) or a reputable aquarium-brand magnesium supplement. 2. Dilute: Mix a small amount thoroughly in a cup of RO/DI water. 3. Dose Slowly: Add the diluted solution to an area of high water flow in your sump or tank over a period of time. 4. Monitor: Test pH frequently during dosing to avoid raising it too quickly. Even though it's slow, overdoing it is still possible. Conclusion and Recommendation for Your Goal For your goal of going from pH 7.3 to 8.2 in a brackish/marine system: · Magnesium hydroxide is not the right tool for this job. Its extremely low solubility makes it impractical to achieve a significant pH shift in a reasonable amount of time. You would need to add a very large amount. · Stick to the previous plan. The hierarchy of solutions remains: 1. Address Salinity First. 2. Maximize Aeration to drive off CO₂. This is often the primary cause of low pH. 3. Test and Raise Carbonate Hardness (KH). This is the most effective and stable method. Use a marine buffer to raise your KH to 8-12 dKH. This will naturally and reliably raise your pH to 8.2-8. 3 and lock it in place. 4. Calcium Hydroxide (Kalkwasser) is a more appropriate active dosing method than magnesium hydroxide if you need to combat consistently low pH after steps 1-3, especially in a reef tank with high calcium demand. In summary: Think of magnesium hydroxide primarily as a magnesium supplement for marine tanks, not as a primary pH-raising agent. For your purpose, boosting KH with a buffer is the simplest, most predictable, and safest method.
