# The Conway medium is a classic and widely used fertilizer formulation for growing marine microalgae in aquaculture hatcheries. It provides a balanced mix of major nutrients (Nitrogen, Phosphorus) and essential trace metals and vitamins. Unlike a single product, "Conway fertilizer" refers to a recipe. You can either purchase it as a pre-made kit or mix it yourself from laboratory-grade chemicals. --- ### What is Conway Medium? It is a defined, chemical solution used to enrich seawater to create the perfect "soup" for growing specific species of microalgae (e.g., *Isochrysis*, *Chaetoceros*, *Tetraselmis*). It was developed by the renowned algal physiologist W. A. P. Conway. ### Composition of Conway Medium The classic recipe is divided into three separate stock solutions to prevent chemicals from reacting with each other and precipitating during storage. --- #### Solution A (Macronutrients) * Function: Provides the main building blocks for algal growth (Nitrogen, Phosphorus). * Typical Composition (per 1 liter of fresh water): * Ammonium Nitrate (NH‚ÇÑNO‚ÇÉ) or Sodium Nitrate (NaNO‚ÇÉ): 50 - 100 g * Sodium Dihydrogen Phosphate (NaH‚ÇÇPO‚ÇÑ ¬∑ 2H‚ÇÇO): 5 - 10 g * Dosage: 1 mL of Solution A is added per 1 liter of algae culture. ---- #### Solution B (Trace Metals + Iron) * Function: Provides essential micronutrients, including chelated iron. * Typical Composition (per 1 liter of fresh water): * Iron Chelate (Fe-EDTA or Fe-Citrate): 5 - 10 g (This is your bioavailable iron source) * Manganese Chloride (MnCl‚ÇÇ ¬∑ 4H‚ÇÇO): 0.5 g * Boric Acid (H‚ÇÉBO‚ÇÉ): 5 g * Zinc Sulfate (ZnSO‚ÇÑ ¬∑ 7H‚ÇÇO): 0.5 g * Cobalt Nitrate (Co(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ ¬∑ 6H‚ÇÇO): 0.1 g * Copper Sulfate (CuSO‚ÇÑ ¬∑ 5H‚ÇÇO): 0.02 g * Sodium Molybdate (Na‚ÇÇMoO‚ÇÑ ¬∑ 2H‚ÇÇO): 0.1 g * Dosage: 1 mL of Solution B is added per 1 liter of algae culture. --- #### Solution C (Vitamins) * Function: Provides essential vitamins that many algae cannot synthesize themselves. * Typical Composition (per 1 liter of fresh water): * Vitamin B12 (Cyanocobalamin): 0.1 g * Vitamin B1 (Thiamine HCl): 1.0 g * Biotin: 0.01 g * Important: This solution is highly perishable. It must be stored in a refrigerator and made fresh every 1-2 months. * Dosage: 1 mL of Solution C is added per 1 liter of algae culture. --- ### How to Use Conway Medium 1. Prepare Stock Solutions: Dissolve the chemicals for Solutions A, B, and C in separate 1-liter bottles of distilled or RO water. 2. Sterilize (Optional but Recommended): Filter each solution through a 0.2-micron filter to prevent bacterial contamination. Solution C should not be autoclaved (heat will destroy the vitamins). 3. Enrich the Seawater: * Fill your algae culture tank with filtered and sterilized seawater. * For every 1 liter of seawater, add: * 1 mL of Solution A * 1 mL of Solution B * 1 mL of Solution C * Example: For a 100-liter culture tank, add 100 mL of each solution. 4. Inoculate: Add your pure algae culture (the "starter") to the fertilized seawater. 5. Aerate and Illuminate: Provide constant light and aeration to promote growth. --- ### Where to Get Conway Fertilizer Products You have two options: #### 1. Pre-Mixed Kits (Recommended for Beginners/Small Operations) This is the easiest and most reliable method. You can buy convenient kits online from aquaculture supply companies. They typically provide pre-measured packets of powders to make each stock solution. Recommended Indonesian & International Suppliers: * PT. Suri Tani Pemuka (Japfa Comfeed): A major Indonesian aquaculture company that likely supplies hatchery inputs. * Local Aquaculture Shops: Check suppliers in Batam or other major aquaculture hubs. They often import or distribute these kits. * International Online Retailers: Websites like AlgaeLab, BrineShrimpDirect, or AquaStock often ship internationally. Search for "Conway Medium kit" or "Algae Fertilizer kit". #### 2. Mix Your Own (For Larger Operations This is an advanced but crucial topic for optimizing microalgae growth. Calculating the final pH of a fertilizer mixture is complex because it involves acid-base chemistry, ion interactions, and the buffering capacity of seawater. However, we can establish a practical framework for managing pH in your hatchery. ### The Core Challenge You are mixing two things: 1. Seawater: A highly buffered solution at ~pH 8.1-8.2. Its carbonate-bicarbonate system (CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª/HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª) resists pH changes. 2. Fertilizer Solutions: These are often acidic (especially the trace metal solution B). The final pH is not a simple average. It depends on the buffering capacity of your specific seawater and the quantity and acidity of the fertilizer added. --- ### Step-by-Step Practical Guide (Instead of Pure Calculation) For a hatchery, a practical, iterative approach is more effective than a theoretical calculation. #### 1. The Goal pH Range Most marine microalgae (e.g., *Chaetoceros*, *Tetraselmis*, *Isochrysis*) thrive in a pH range of 7.8 - 8.5. #### 2. The Practical Method: Measure and Adjust This is the most reliable method for a hatchery setting. What you need: * A calibrated pH meter (essential tool). * A fresh batch of your filtered seawater. * Your stock fertilizer solutions (Conway A, B, C). Procedure: 1. Measure Initial pH: Take a 1-liter sample of your filtered seawater. Measure and record its pH. (e.g., pH 8.2) 2. Add Full Fertilizer Dose: Add the standard dose of your fertilizers to the 1-liter sample (e.g., 1 mL of Solution A, 1 mL of Solution B, 1 mL of Solution C). Stir well. 3. Measure Final pH: Wait a few minutes for the reading to stabilize, then measure the pH again. (e.g., It might drop to pH 7.9). 4. Interpret the Result: * If pH is within 7.8-8.5 (e.g., 7.9): Perfect. Proceed with inoculating your algae. * If pH is too low (< 7.8): Your fertilizer mix is too acidic for your local seawater's buffering capacity. * If pH is too high (> 8.5): Unlikely when adding fertilizer, but possible if your source water is unusual. #### 3. How to Adjust the Final pH If pH is too LOW after fertilization: * Solution: Dilute the Fertilizer Dose. * This is the safest and most common method. * Example: If adding 1 mL/L of each solution caused a larger-than-desired drop, try adding only 0.7 mL/L of Solution B (the most acidic one, containing trace metals). Keep Solutions A and C at 1 mL/L. * Re-test the pH with this new ratio. Find the dose that gives you the target pH. Algae growth might be slightly slower but more stable than in a low-pH, stressful environment. If adjustment is absolutely necessary (use cautiously): * To Raise pH (if too low): Use a very dilute solution of Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) or Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO‚ÇÉ). Add dropwise to the fertilized seawater while stirring and monitoring the pH meter. Bicarbonate is safer and adds carbon, which algae can use. * To Lower pH (if too high): Use a very dilute solution of Hydrochloric Acid (HCl). This is rarely needed after fertilizing. Warning: Avoid using these strong acids/bases directly in your main culture tank. Always adjust a small sample first to determine the required amount. --- ### Understanding the "Why": The Chemistry of Conway Solutions * Solution A (Macronutrients): Generally near neutral pH. Has minimal impact. * Solution B (Trace Metals): This is the most acidic solution. The metal salts (especially Iron-EDTA) hydrolyze in water, releasing H‚Å∫ ions and lowering the pH significantly. * Solution C (Vitamins): Minimal impact on pH. ### Example Workflow for a New Seawater Source Let's say you have a new batch of seawater and a standard Conway recipe. 1. Seawater pH = 8.2 2. Add 1 mL/L each of A, B, C. 3. Final pH = 7.6 *(Too low!)* 4. Action: Create a new test liter. Add 1 mL/L of A, 0.5 mL/L of B, and 1 mL/L of C. 5. New Final pH = 7.9 *(Acceptable!)* 6.
